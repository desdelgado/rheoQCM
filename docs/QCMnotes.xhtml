1 Background Theory

As the name implies, the quartz crystal microbalance (QCM) is most often employed as a high sensitivity mass-balance. The instrument relies on the piezoelectric nature of quartz to measure changes in resonance frequency caused by the application of a material to the quartz surface. An adsorbed mass or deposited film results in a decrease in resonant frequency of the quartz crystal by an amount, Δ f sn , that is given by the well-known Sauerbrey expression[sauerbrey_verwendung_1959]: Δ f sn = 2n f 1 2 Z q Δ M A = 2n f 1 2 Z q ρ f d f (1) Here, n is the order of the resonance harmonic, f 1 is the fundamental resonance frequency of quartz (5 MHz), Z q is its acoustic shear impedance (8.84 × 1 0 6 kg/ m 2 s for AT cut quartz), Δ M A is the mass per unit area of the film, ρ is its density and d is its thickness. Note that ρ d is the mass per unit area.
Originally, the technique was designed for use in an air or vacuum environment, but today the QCM is often used to characterize molecules, particles and thin-films in liquid media. Applications include studies of adsorption[marx_quartz_2003, cooper_survey_2007, becker_survey_2011, tiraferri_mechanism_2014, yi_release_2014, song_adsorption_2014, salama_adsorption_2014, rudrake_combined_2009, liu_quartz_2011], self-assembly[marx_quartz_2003, cooper_survey_2007, becker_survey_2011], cell-substrate interactions[wegener_quartz_2001], and electrochemistry[buttry_measurement_1992, hillman_electrochemical_2003].
The QCM consists of a single-crystal quartz disc sandwiched between two electrodes. Due to the piezoelectric nature of quartz, the material will oscillate transversely if an alternating potential is applied across the electrodes, propagating a shear wave through the disc. If the frequency of oscillation is near to the acoustic resonance frequency of quartz, a standing shear wave will generate across the disc, inducing a peak in the system conductance.[johannsmann_viscoelastic_2008, denolf_high_2011] For the AT-cut crystals used throughout this work, the fundamental resonant frequency is near 5 MHz. The conductance also peaks at odd harmonics (n) of the fundamental resonant frequency, with resonances at 15 MHz (n=3), 25 MHz (n=5), etc. If the conductance is plotted in the frequency domain, Lorentzian curves can be fit to the conductance peaks to provide two values: the resonance frequency ( f n ), where the conductance is maximum at the n th harmonic, and the half-bandwidth ( Γ n ) , the half-width at half-maximum of the conductance peak. This half-bandwidth is equal to 2D/ f n , where D is the dissipation factor introduced by the Chalmers group and utilized in the commonly employed time-domain QCM-D technique[voinova_viscoelastic_1999]. We refer to Γ n simply as the dissipation, recognizing that our definition of the dissipation differs from the QCM-D definition by a factor of 2/ f n .
If a load is applied to the QCM surface, f n and Γ n shift relative to the bare crystal and together make up a complex frequency shift[johannsmann_viscoelastic_2008, denolf_high_2011]: Δ f n * = Δ f n +i Δ Γ n (2)
Note that in our notation, starred quantities are complex, and quantities subscripted with an n depend on the harmonic at which they are measured. When the frequency shift is small in comparison to the resonance frequency of quartz, which is true in nearly all applications of the QCM, Δ f n * is related to the shift in complex load impedance ( Δ Z nL * ) through the small load approximation[johannsmann_viscoelastic_2008]: Δ f n * = i f 1 Δ Z nL * π Z q (3)

1.1 Sauerbrey Limit

The Sauerbrey equation (Eq. 1) is obtained from Eq. 3, using the following inertial load impedance, Δ Z ns * , for Δ Z nL * :
Δ Z sn * =2 π in f 1 ρ f d f (4)

1.2 Bulk Limit

For thicker films, the complex resonant frequency deviates from the Sauerbrey relationship and depends on the rheological properties of the material in contact with the crystal surface. For films that are sufficiently thick (well beyond the decay length of the acoustic shear wave), the load impedance is equal to the acoustic impedance of a bulk material: Z n,bulk * =( ρ G n * ) 1/2 (5) Here G n * is the complex shear modulus at f n , expressed here in terms of its magnitude and phase angle:
G n * =| G n * | exp ( i φ n ) (6)
Use of this Z n * for the load impedance gives the following for Δ f n and Δ Γ n :
Δ f n = f 1 π Z q ( ρ | G n * | ) 1/2 sin ( φ n /2 ) (7)
Δ Γ n = f 1 π Z q ( ρ | G n * | ) 1/2 cos ( φ n /2 ) (8)
These equations can be inverted to give the following for ρ | G n * | and φ
ρ | G n * | = ( π Z q | Δ f n * | f 1 ) 2 (9)
φ =2 arctan ( Δ f n Δ Γ n ) (10)

1.3 Generalized Single Layer

The complex wave vector, k n * , for shear wave propagation in the material can be obtained directly from Z n * [simonetti_nature_2004]:
k n * = 2 π f n ρ Z n * (11)
The wavelength of the propagating shear wave is then obtained from the real part of k * [simonetti_nature_2004]:
λ n = 2 π Re ( k n * ) = 1 f n ( | G n * | ρ ) 1/2 1 cos ( φ n /2 ) (12)
or
λ n ρ = ( ρ | G n * | ) 1/2 f n cos ( φ n /2 ) (13)
Finally, we define a quantity, D n * , which plays an important role in the expressions given below: D n * = k n * d= 2 π d λ n ( 1-i tan ( φ n /2 ) ) = 2 π f n d ρ Z n * (14)
The impedance of a layer (which we refer to hear as layer 1) of mass thickness d ρ is given by the following expression:
Z ni,film * =i Z ni,bulk * tan ( D n1 * ) (15)

1.4 Extension to 2 Layers

Figure 1: Two-layer geometry involving a 'film' that is sandwiched between the electrode surface and a 'membrane'.
In a variety of situations we are interested in two layer geometries, where the film we are interested in is sandwiched between the electrode surface of the QCM and a second layer, which we refer to the membrane layer. In our notation the acoustic shear impedance of the membrane layer at the nth harmonic is Z nm * , k nm * is the corresponding wave vector and d m is the membrane thickness. The overall impedance of this two-layer system is (Eq. 18 from Johannsmann's 1999 paper) [johannsmann_viscoelastic_1999]:
Z n12 * = iZ n1 * Z n1 * tan ( D n1 * ) + Z n2 * tan ( D n2 * ) Z n1 * - Z n2 * tan ( D n1 * ) tan ( D n2 * ) (16)
We can write this in a more useful way by writing the membrane impedance, in the following way (following our previous notation) [brass_contact_2006, brass_membrane-enhanced_2008]:
Z nm,0 * =i Z nm * tan ( k nm * d m ) (17)
The difference between the 2-layer impedance and the impedance of the membrane layer itself is defined as:
Δ Z nfm * ≡ Z nf * i Z n1,bulk * tan ( D n1 * ) + Z n2,film * Z n1,bulk * +i Z n2,film * tan ( D n1 * ) - Z n2,film * (18)
We can apply Eq. 3, with Δ Z nL * = Δ Z nfm * to get an expression for the complex frequency shift. After a bunch of algebra, we get to the following form of the master equation:
Δ f n * f sn =- tan ( D n1 * ) D n1 * [ 1- ( r nfm * ) 2 1+i r n12 * tan ( D nf * ) ] (19)
Where r * is the ratio of the overall membrane impedance to the material impedance for the film:
r n12 * = Z n2,film * Z n1,bulk * (20)
Written in this way it's a bit more obvious that there is no net response for the trivial case when a thin film and thick membrane have identical properties ( r nfm * =1 ), and that the single layer master equation is recovered for experiments done in air ( r nfm * =0 ).
Note that we define Δ f sn as a positive number (see Eq. 1), so in the case where the Sauerbrey limit applies ( d/ λ n ≪ 1) , and when there is sufficient acoustic contrast between the film and the surrounding liquid ( | Z nf * | ≫ | Z n ℓ * | ), we have Δ f n * =- Δ f sn .

1.4.1 Special Case - Film Immersed in Liquid

In a liquid medium, the 'membrane' actually corresponds to a bulk, infinitely thick liquid. The impedance ratio is now a ratio of pure material properties:
r nf m * = ( ρ m G nm * ρ f G nf * ) 1/2 (21)
In the past, we have expressed Eq. 19 in the following way: Δ f n * Δ f sn =- D nf *-2 + R n ℓ *2 cot ( D nf * ) / D nf * + R n ℓ * (22)
Here R n ℓ * defines the ratio of the complex frequency shift for the liquid to the Sauerbrey frequency shift for the film:
R n ℓ * = Δ f n ℓ +i Δ Γ n ℓ Δ f sn (23)

If we do a temperature scan from low temperatures (where the film is glassy and the Sauerbrey expression works for everything), then subtract the low temperature value of f n * from the one at higher temperatures to obtain a quantity we call Δ f nT * , which from Eq. 28 can be written in the following way:

3 Solution Method and Error Analysis

The numerical solution obtains three quantities (typically d ρ , | G 3 ρ | and φ ) that give predicted three measured quantities in agreement with experimental values. These three measured quantities are two frequency shifts and one dissipation shift. In a n 1 : n 2 , n 3 calculation, these quantities are Δ f n1 , Δ f n 2 and Δ Γ n 3 . MATLAB and Python both return the Jacobian, J , at the solution point, which is given by the following expression:
J=[ ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ φ ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ φ ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ∂ φ ] (34)
Once we have the matrix, we invert it to get J -1 , the Jacobian of the inverse function, which is what we need:
J inv ≡ J -1 =[ ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ ( d ρ ) ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ ( | G 3 | ρ ) ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ∂ φ ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ φ ∂ Δ f n 2 ∂ φ ∂ Δ Γ n 3 ] (35)
The uncertainties in the experimental quantities are Δ f n1 err , Δ f n 2 err and Δ Γ n3 err . We represent these as three component error vector:
err={ Δ f n1 err Δ f n2 err Δ Γ n3 err } (36)
In our linearized analysis we just multiply the uncertainties by the appropriate partial derivatives, and sum the three different uncertainties in quadrature to get the associated experimental uncertainty in our extracted property:
( d ρ ) err = ( ( J 11 inv err( 1 ) ) 2 + ( J 12 inv err( 2 ) ) 2 + ( J 13 inv err( 3 ) ) 2 ) 0.5 ( | G 3 | ρ ) err = ( ( J 21 inv err( 1 ) ) 2 + ( J 22 inv err( 2 ) ) 2 + ( J 23 inv err( 3 ) ) 2 ) 0.5 φ err = ( ( J 31 inv err( 1 ) ) 2 + ( J 32 inv err( 2 ) ) 2 + ( J 33 inv err( 3 ) ) 2 ) 0.5 (37)
This same analysis can be applied to more complicated models as well.

4 Some Other Expressions

We can simplify the area analysis considerably by writing a single function with three equations and three unknowns. For an x:y,z calculation the variables are d ρ , G ρ 3 and φ . The equations just equate the measured and predicted values of Δ f x , Δ f y and Δ Γ z . Right now many of our expressions are in terms of d/ λ , so it's convenient to write this in terms of the variables that we ultimately care about:
d/ λ n =d ρ f n ( 1 ρ | G n * | ) 1/2 cos ( φ n /2 ) (38) The inverse relationship is:
ρ | G n * | = ( d ρ n f 1 cos ( φ n /2 ) ( d/ λ n ) ) 2 (39)
This leads to the following expression for D n *
D n * = k n * d= 2 π d ρ f n ( ρ | G n * | ) 1/2 ( cos ( φ n /2 ) -i sin ( φ n /2 ) ) = 2 π f n d ρ Z n * (40)
We'll use the constant phase approximation, so φ is assumed to be independent of n . We'll use G 3 * as our reference, using the following expression for | G n * | :
| G n * | =| G 3 * | ( n/3 ) φ /90 (41)
d λ n = d λ 3 ( n 3 ) 1- φ /180 (42)
We can also get a relationship between φ and d λ at a fixed value of J n ′ ′ , which helps us put the thin film limit in the context of the contour plots for r h and r d :
d/ λ n =d ρ n f 1 ( | J n * | sin φ n ρ ) 1/2 cos ( φ n /2 ) ( sin φ n ) 1/2
The following expression for the load impedance of a film is also useful, which combines Eqs. 11 and 17:
Z n,0 * =i Z n * tan ( 2 π n f 1 ρ d Z n * ) (43)
We can also relate the resonance frequency to the crystal thickness, starting with the shear wave velocity in quartz.
c q = ( G q ρ q ) 1/2 = 1 ρ q ( G q ρ q ) 1/2 = Z q ρ q
This velocity is then related to the fundamental resonant frequency and the thickness through the following expression:
d q = c q 2 f 1 = Z q 2 ρ q f 1 ; d q ρ q = Z q 2 f 1
D nq * = 2 π n f 1 d q ρ q Z q = π n (44)

4.1 Voigt Model

In the power law model commonly employed in our group, φ is assumed to be independent of n and the n -dependence of d/ λ n is given by Eq. 42. In the commonly used Voigt model, G ′ is assumed to be independent of n and G ′ ′ is assumed to increase linearly with n :
G n ′ = G 3 ′ =| G 3 * | cos φ 3 (45)
G n ′ ′ = n 3 G 3 ′ ′ = n 3 | G 3 * | sin φ 3 (46)
This model is not rheologically self-consistent, as pointed out by Reviakine et al. [reviakine_hearing_2011] However, we can use it as a point of comparison to see how much the assumed frequency dependence affects the properties that are extracted from our analysis. Equations 45 and 46 can be combined to give the following expressions for | G n * | and φ n :
φ n = arctan ( n 3 tan φ 3 ) (47)
| G n * | =| G 3 * | ( cos 2 ( φ 3 ) + n 2 9 sin 2 ( φ 3 ) ) 1/2 (48)
These expressions can then be used in conjunction with Eq. 38 to get an expression relating d/ λ n to d/ λ 3 and φ 3 :
d λ n = d λ 3 n 3 ( cos 2 ( φ 3 ) + n 2 9 sin 2 ( φ 3 ) ) -1/4 cos ( 1 2 arctan ( n 3 tan φ 3 ) ) cos ( φ 3 /2 ) (49)

5 Error determination for J 3 ′ ′

For thin films, we are most sensitive to J 3 ′ ′ = sin φ /| G 3 * | . Equivalently, we have:
J 3 ′ ′ / ρ = sin φ /| G 3 * | ρ (50)
or
| G 3 * | ρ = sin φ /( J 3 ′ ′ / ρ ) (51)
From the chain rule, we have
∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( J ′ ′ / ρ ) = ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( | G 3 * | ρ ) ∂ ( | G 3 * | ρ ) ∂ ( J ′ ′ / ρ ) =- ∂ Δ f n 1 ∂ ( | G 3 * | ρ ) sin φ ( J 3 ′ ′ / ρ ) 2 (52)
So to get the Jacobian for for the case where | G 3 * | ρ is replaced by J ′ ′ / ρ we just multiply the middle row in the Jacobian by - sin φ / ( J 3 ′ ′ / ρ ) 2 .
